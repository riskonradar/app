# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Reliability engineers and quality engineers who work on failure mode analysis, root cause investigation, and operational risk assessment. They use Risk on Radar to transform fragmented engineering failure evidence into structured, traceable reliability intelligence. Their context is professional engineering work - they need precision, auditability, and efficiency, but the interface should feel modern and approachable rather than academic or overly complex.

## Product Purpose

Risk on Radar is an evidence-backed FMEA intelligence platform that helps reliability and quality engineering teams turn fragmented failure evidence into adaptive reliability intelligence. The product indexes peer-reviewed failure literature and engineering evidence into structured reliability knowledge, then surfaces that knowledge inside engineering workflows like FMEA building, root-cause analysis, and predictive maintenance. Success is when engineers can efficiently discover, validate, and export traceable reliability data that integrates into their existing engineering processes.

## Brand Personality

Approachable technical. The interface should feel modern and innovative while remaining precise and engineering-focused. Not overly academic or dry, but also not sacrificing technical depth for approachability. The tone is confident and capable without being intimidating - sophisticated engineering tools that feel human-centered.

## Anti-references

Avoid SaaS clichés. The interface should not look like typical SaaS dashboards with navy/white color schemes, endless card grids, or hero-metric templates. Avoid traditional engineering software aesthetics (old MATLAB interfaces, basic Excel-like grids, legacy CAD tools). The goal is a professional engineering workspace that feels contemporary and purpose-built, not a generic template or a legacy tool.

## Design Principles

1. **Evidence over opinion**: Every reliability claim should be traceable to source evidence. The UI should make evidence lineage visible and auditable.
2. **Workflow efficiency**: Engineers use this to get work done. Reduce friction in data entry, filtering, and export while maintaining precision.
3. **Contextual clarity**: Complex failure data should be organized so engineers can quickly find what's relevant to their specific component, system, or operating context.
4. **Professional confidence**: The interface should inspire trust through precision, consistency, and attention to detail without feeling sterile or overly academic.
5. **Human-centered engineering**: Balance technical depth with approachable design. Engineering tools can feel modern and thoughtful without sacrificing capability.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AAA compliance. The interface must meet the highest accessibility standards, ensuring all engineers can use the tool effectively regardless of assistive technology needs. This includes robust keyboard navigation, comprehensive screen reader support, high contrast ratios, and accommodations for reduced motion preferences. Professional engineering tools should be inclusive by default.
