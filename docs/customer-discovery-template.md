# Customer Discovery Template

Use this for 10-20 conversations before investor outreach. The goal is not to pitch hard. The goal is to learn whether reliability and quality teams have a painful, frequent, budget-backed problem.

## Target Participants

- Reliability engineer
- Quality engineer
- FMEA owner
- Failure analysis engineer
- Maintenance or asset integrity lead
- Engineering manager responsible for safety/reliability workflows

## Call Structure

### 1. Context

Ask:
- What kinds of assets or systems are you responsible for?
- Where do FMEA, RCA, failure investigation, or reliability reviews show up in your work?
- How often do you update or review this information?

### 2. Current Workflow

Ask:
- Walk me through the last time you had to research a failure mode or update an FMEA.
- What tools did you use?
- Where did the evidence come from?
- Who reviewed or approved the result?
- How long did it take?

### 3. Pain

Ask:
- What is the most annoying or risky part of that workflow?
- What gets missed?
- What creates rework?
- What is hard to defend during review, audit, or customer discussions?
- If this problem stayed the same for another year, what would it cost you?

### 4. Budget And Buying

Ask:
- Is this problem important enough that the company pays for tools, consultants, or databases today?
- Who owns the budget for this workflow?
- What would need to be true for you to trial a new tool?
- What would make procurement or IT say no?

### 5. Product Reaction

Show the demo only after understanding the workflow.

Ask:
- Which part feels useful?
- Which part feels wrong or untrusted?
- What evidence would you need before relying on this?
- Would you use this for a real FMEA/reliability task?
- Who else should we speak with?

## Score Each Conversation

Rate 1-5:
- Pain severity
- Frequency
- Budget signal
- Urgency
- Product fit
- Access to a pilot workflow

## Strong Signal

A strong conversation ends with one of:
- They introduce another stakeholder.
- They share a real workflow/example.
- They ask for a pilot.
- They discuss budget or procurement.
- They want to see the product again with a colleague.

## Weak Signal

A weak conversation sounds like:
- "Interesting."
- "Could be useful someday."
- "Send me a deck."
- They cannot name a recent painful workflow.
- They cannot say who would pay.
